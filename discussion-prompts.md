# Discussion Prompts

Questions for the group. No right answers — just things worth thinking about.

---

**1. Where are you on the spectrum?**

Are you mostly in assistant mode (ask, copy, paste), or have you started orchestrating AI workflows? What pushed you to change — or what's keeping you where you are?

**2. When does "writing code" stop being the job?**

If AI can reliably generate implementation from a clear spec, what do senior engineers actually spend their time on? Is that a good thing?

**3. What does architecture mean when AI is the builder?**

Traditionally, architecture meant system design documents, diagrams, and tech lead decisions. Does it now include things like `CLAUDE.md` files, permission configs, and test suites designed to constrain AI behavior?

**4. Where does human judgment still matter most?**

AI can iterate on code until tests pass. But what are the decisions that still require human experience, taste, or context that an AI can't get from the repo alone?

**5. How does your repo change when AI agents participate?**

Do you need more explicit specs? Stricter test coverage? Different file organization? What would you add to a repo today if you knew an AI agent would be working in it tomorrow?

**6. What are the risks of autonomous AI loops?**

Agent loops can run without supervision. What could go wrong? How do you think about security, blast radius, and guardrails when an AI can execute shell commands in your project?

**7. Is "AI team manager" a real role?**

If orchestrating AI agents becomes a core skill, does that change job titles, team structures, or how we hire? Is managing AI agents fundamentally different from managing human engineers?

**8. What skills become more valuable?**

If implementation gets cheaper, what gets more expensive? System thinking? Taste? Domain knowledge? Communication? Testing? Something else?

**9. What are you not comfortable delegating to AI yet?**

Is there a task you've tried to hand off to AI and pulled back? What made you uncomfortable — trust, quality, control, or something else?

**10. What does "senior engineer" mean in 2026?**

Five years ago, it meant deep technical expertise and the ability to ship complex features. Has that definition changed? Should it?
